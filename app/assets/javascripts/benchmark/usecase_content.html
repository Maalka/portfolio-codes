Municipal Portfolio Codes Assessment Use Case

Cities across the country are beginning to explore ways to improve the energy efficiency of their building portfolios. The process of identifying specific improvement strategies can be long and confusing, spanning multiple levels of analysis. The Portfolio Codes Assessment tool was designed to be the first step, by enabling municipalities to conduct a quick, simple analysis to understand the typical breakdown of energy end-uses for their buildings and the impact that different tiers of investment strategies may have on their overall energy-use and efficiency.

How was the Portfolio Codes Tool created?

The MPP team worked with the cities of Providence (RI), Grand Rapids (MI), and Eugene (OR) to develop and refine a process for data-driven Strategic Energy Management. The cities were chosen because they could offer perspectives into how energy-usage patterns may vary across distinct climate zones.

Over time, the team was able to work with each city to collect energy-use data for each building in its portfolio. The team normalized the data by building size and grouped buildings by type to statistically determine a representative energy-use profile for each building type in the portfolio (See Below - Red lines correspond to representative building).

The representative building energy-use profile was then combined with actual meteorological (AMY) data to articulate and calibrate a precise energy model for the most common building types found in a municipal portfolio. The breakdown of energy end-uses was determined for each building type.

Finally, several scenarios for improving energy efficiency were tested on the representative energy models and results were integrated into the open-source tool.

What were the scenarios tested on the calibrated models?

4 tiers of improvement strategies, increasing in cost and comprehensiveness, were then applied to the calibrated models and impacts were analyzed. Outputs of these calibrated models and simulation were finally integrated into this open-source tool to enable users to understand differences in how buildings across climate zones consume energy and how improvement strategies can impact performance across a portfolio.
